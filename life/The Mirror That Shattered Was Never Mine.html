The Mirror That Shattered Was Never Mine

(A lamentation, an unmasking, a resurrection)

I. Invocation of Quiet Men

They say nothing is louder than the silence of a man
who listens without flinching—
whose love does not shout,
but simply remains.

I stood, bare-palmed,
not armored in vengeance, not sculpted in flame,
but softened —
hands open like parentheses
to hold the weight of a story
you said you wanted me to hear.

You spoke of ghosts,
and I did not interrupt the funeral procession.
I brushed my hand along your shoulder,
not to fix the wreckage,
but to say, "I see the house still smoldering. I will not walk away."

But you did not want presence.
You wanted a war cry.

You mistook quiet empathy
for the apathy of men
who cannot name sorrow without breaking things.

II. Where Guilt Was Passed Like Inheritance

You told me I made you feel small.
But it was not my voice that cast the shadow —
it was the architecture of your own shame.

You held out riddles,
disguised them as conversations,
and when I did not solve them
in the exact cadence you imagined,
you punished me for being honest
instead of clairvoyant.

I gave you every version of my heart
that language could stretch to hold.
I wrote essays for questions never truly asked,
offered metaphors in place of apologies I never owed,
sat inside guilt dressed in your perfume,
wondering if I was the one who had gone cold.

But the fire was yours,
and I only ever tried to bring water.

III. The Inversion

You said I made you cut.
That I was cold.
That I did not carry your pain gently enough.
But I have walked through flame for you
while you watched from the shade,
wondering why I was sweating.

You drank to speak to me.
You smoked to soften your own voice.
I gave up wine and you gave me disbelief—
as if sobriety were a betrayal
and not a boundary I bled to hold.

And even in the parting,
you placed your sadness into my hands
as if I had carved it.

IV. The Chorus of Echoes

Your friends—not many stayed.
Your little sister-a mirror you couldn’t look into.
Your mother—a wound you called an enemy.

I was merely next.

The pattern was older than me—
I was just the most recent recipient
of your unfinished grief.

I wanted to love you like a conversation,
but you wanted to be worshipped like an apology.

You said, "Why didn’t you say what my father would say?"
And I replied, "Because I am not your father. I am not the war. I am not the sword."
But you didn’t hear that.
You only heard silence
where a performance should have been.

V. Resurrection in the Aftermath

I am not sorry for being gentle.
I am not sorry for being still.
I am not sorry for not screaming when you wanted thunder
or for not bleeding theatrics at your altar.

I will not carry guilt for loving with integrity
simply because you were unfamiliar with quiet kindness.

My hands were not empty —
they were holding presence, not spectacle.

I leave you not because I lacked devotion,
but because I will no longer explain my softness
to people who only recognize love by the damage it leaves behind.

VI. Coda — Blackbird Ascending

Ahh—
I flew without wings, fell through flame,
and still I rise through the wreckage of your name.

I bled for a silence you never heard,
sang for a love you never returned.

Now I rise, unbound,
from the echo of your grief.
No chains left on my voice,
no weight left on my speech.

Epilogue — The Sermon I Never Gave

She said,
"An ungodly man is a dealbreaker."

And I listened.
Not to argue. Not to defend.
But to understand the altar she prayed at
and whether I was ever welcome to kneel there.

So I opened my hands,
not with incense or scripture,
but with history —
a liturgy written in the margins of my life,
not in verses, but in scars.

I brought her the essay,
the long hymn of my journey —
not toward her god,
but toward my own peace.

And still, she called it betrayal.

She did not want understanding.
She wanted exception.
She did not want a man to stand in his truth —
She wanted one to discard it quietly
for the comfort of her conditions.

If godliness is the price of your affection,
then love was never the offering —
only a sermon dressed as safety.

And I would rather walk alone,
unblessed but whole,
than bow in a place
that demands I abandon my soul.
